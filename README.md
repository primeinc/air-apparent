## Technical Foundations for an Interactive Indoor Airflow Simulator (Godot 4 + GPU Thermal LBM) — MVP SSOT Research Brief

## TL;DR

- **Feasible on both target GPUs.** A single Godot 4 app running a GPU thermal LBM solver (D3Q19 velocity + D3Q7 thermal DDF, Boussinesq buoyancy, Smagorinsky LES) for a ~5×5×2.5 m room at ~2 cm resolution (≈256×256×128 ≈ 8.4M cells) is well within the memory and throughput budgets of both the RTX 3070 (8 GB) and RTX 5060 Ti (16 GB). VRAM is not the binding constraint; interactive step-rate and numerical stability are.
- **You must write your own solver.** FluidX3D is the performance/technique reference (Esoteric-Pull in-place streaming, FP32/FP16 memory, 55 B/cell, ~5,100 MLUPs FP16 on a 3070) but is licensed free-for-non-commercial-use only  — unusable in a paid product. Port the *techniques*, not the code; a permissive reference (Autodesk XLB, Apache-2.0) exists, but a bespoke Godot compute-shader solver is the cleanest path.
- **Two subsystems carry the real risk:** (1) the Gaussian-splat → simulation-domain pipeline (splat-to-watertight-geometry for indoor rooms is still research-grade and needs cleanup), and (2) physics-credibility framing (ship as “plausible airflow visualization,” validate against IEA Annex 20 / heated-cavity, and explicitly disclaim engineering-grade prediction).

## Key Findings

### Subsystem 1 — Solver Core (GPU thermal LBM): FEASIBLE on both GPUs

- **Prior art is directly on-point.** Delbosc et al. (University of Leeds) implemented real-time indoor-airflow LBM on GPU with exactly the ingredients proposed: D3Q19, Smagorinsky LES for turbulence, and a coupled second LBM lattice for temperature under the Boussinesq approximation. Delbosc’s 2015 PhD thesis (White Rose eTheses) states “the program kernel is shown to achieve a performance of up to 1.5 billion lattice node updates per second, which is found to be sufficient for coarse real-time simulations.” (The earlier Delbosc et al. 2014 paper in *Computers & Mathematics with Applications* reported ~half a billion lattice-node updates/s.) Khan, Delbosc, Noakes & Summers (*Building Simulation* 8(4), 2015) validated LBM indoor results against CFD-LES: “The predicted results from LBM are compared with traditional CFD based large eddy simulations (LES). Reasonable agreement between LBM results and LES method is observed with significantly faster computational times.”
- **Thermal coupling approach:** The double-distribution-function (DDF) method is the standard, most stable approach — one distribution for momentum (D3Q19), a second smaller lattice (commonly D3Q7 or D3Q5) for temperature, coupled via a Boussinesq buoyancy force term. The LBM literature explicitly notes the DDF approach “keeps the intrinsic features and simple structures” of LBM and “is numerically more stable” than single high-order-lattice thermal models. This suits low-velocity, buoyancy-driven indoor flow.
- **Turbulence:** Smagorinsky LES subgrid model is the established choice and integrates cleanly into LBM by adding an eddy viscosity to the relaxation time (as both FluidX3D and Delbosc do). Caveat: standard Smagorinsky is over-dissipative in low-Reynolds, stratified, buoyancy-driven regimes — acceptable for a “plausible visualization” tool but a documented accuracy limitation. (Vreman and WALE subgrid models, used in Molla et al.’s LBM-LES validation against the Posner partitioned-room experiment, are lower-dissipation alternatives if needed.)
- **Performance headroom (hardened numbers, FluidX3D official benchmark table, github.com/ProjectPhysX/FluidX3D):**
  - RTX 3070 (448 GB/s): **2,578 MLUPs** (FP32/FP32), **5,096 MLUPs** (FP32/FP16S), 88% roofline efficiency. Roofline ceiling at 448 GB/s = 2,928 / 5,818 MLUPs.
  - RTX 3060 Ti (also 448 GB/s): **2,644 / 5,129 MLUPs** — a good proxy for the RTX 5060 Ti 16 GB, which shares 448 GB/s (GDDR7) and is not yet in the benchmark table but should meet or exceed these figures on Blackwell.
  - Since LBM is purely memory-bandwidth-bound and both target cards have identical 448 GB/s bandwidth, **raw solver throughput is nearly identical on the two cards** — the 5060 Ti’s advantage is VRAM capacity (larger grids), not speed.
- **Step-rate math (the number that matters for “real time”):** For an 8.4M-cell room grid at ~5,000 MLUPs (FP16, isothermal), raw solver ≈ 600 steps/s. Adding thermal DDF + LES + moving-geometry re-voxelization and sharing the GPU with rendering realistically cuts effective throughput by ~2–4× (an engineering estimate to confirm empirically), landing at **~150–300 solver steps/s** — comfortably enough to run several LBM steps per rendered 30–60 fps frame and show airflow responding to moved objects within a fraction of a second.

### Subsystem 1 — Memory Budget (VRAM is NOT the constraint)

- FluidX3D’s Esoteric-Pull in-place streaming + FP16 memory compression reaches **55 bytes/cell**  (isothermal, no extensions): per the official README, “a single lattice cell requires: a memory capacity of 93 (FP32/FP32) or 55 (FP32/FP16) Bytes … a memory bandwidth of 153 (FP32/FP32) or 77 (FP32/FP16) Bytes per time step … performance is only limited by memory bandwidth.” This is ~⅙ of a conventional FP64 LBM solver; a 12 GB card fits ~228M cells.
- **Our solver (D3Q19 + thermal D3Q7 DDF, mixed precision) estimate: ~70–80 bytes/cell.** Resulting grid budgets:
  - 128³ (2.1M cells) ≈ 0.15 GB
  - 192³ (7.1M cells) ≈ 0.55 GB
  - 256³ (16.8M cells) ≈ 1.3 GB
  - 384³ (56.6M cells) ≈ 4.2 GB (fits 8 GB with rendering headroom)
  - 512³ (134M cells) ≈ 10 GB (needs the 16 GB card)
- **Verdict:** For the target ~5×5×2.5 m room at 2 cm resolution (≈8.4M cells, ~0.7 GB), both cards have massive headroom. The 8 GB RTX 3070 comfortably runs everything up to ~384³; the 16 GB RTX 5060 Ti unlocks 512³-class grids (≈1 cm cells) if desired.

### Subsystem 1 — Moving/Dynamic Geometry: FEASIBLE, well-trodden

- FluidX3D added GPU voxelization of moving/rotating geometry with automatic per-cell velocity initialization from center-of-rotation + linear + rotational velocity; “cells that are converted from solid→fluid during re-voxelization now have their DDFs properly initialized” (the “refill” problem). STL voxelization completes in milliseconds.
- Standard technique stack: inside/outside voxelization → bounce-back boundary conditions at solid cells → momentum-exchange for forces → DDF refill for newly-uncovered cells. Moving boundaries are currently **one-way** (geometry drives fluid) in FluidX3D — exactly what “move your couch and watch the air respond” needs; no two-way fluid-structure interaction is required for MVP.

### Subsystem 2 — Godot 4 Compute / RenderingDevice: FEASIBLE, some sharp edges

- Godot 4.x exposes a mature compute path: `RenderingServer.create_local_rendering_device()`  (or the main device), GLSL compute shaders compiled to SPIR-V, storage buffers and 3D storage textures (`TEXTURE_TYPE_3D`), uniform sets, and barriers.
- **Zero-copy visualization works:** The official Compute Texture Demo shows a compute shader writing a texture that a material shader then samples in the same frame, using the *main* rendering device and a `Texture2DRD` resource — no CPU readback. The 3D equivalent (3D storage texture sampled by a volumetric raymarch shader) is the intended architecture for sampling the LBM velocity/temperature fields directly from VRAM.
- **Known pitfalls flagged by the community:**
  - Do NOT set `TEXTURE_USAGE_CPU_READ_BIT` casually — reported to cause error -8 (feature not present) on Godot’s Vulkan version.
  - RID lifetime management is manual (buffers, uniforms, pipelines, textures, shaders all leak if not freed).
  - **Language choice matters a lot for dispatch:** GDScript-driven dispatch is fine; the C# path has a documented severe bug (issue #95521: compute dispatch 300–500 ms via C# `rd.Submit()` vs <5 ms via GDScript for the same GLSL). GDExtension C++ has also been reported (godot-cpp #1063) as *not* automatically faster than GDScript for engine-method-heavy loops. **Recommendation:** drive dispatch from GDScript (or a thin C++ GDExtension) but keep ALL heavy work inside the GLSL compute kernels — the host language barely matters if it only issues dispatches.
- **Volumetric rendering options in Godot 4:** (a) raymarched volume shader sampling the 3D texture (most control, best for temperature/velocity volume viz — see MangoButtermilch’s open volumetric renderer and community raymarch tutorials); (b) `FogVolume` with a custom fog shader (easy, limited shape control); (c) `GPUParticles3D` seeded/advected by sampling the flow-field 3D texture for streamline/tracer viz. Note: rendering `GPUParticles3D` as a *true volume* is a still-open Godot proposal (#9653), so use raymarch for the volume and particles-as-tracers, not particles-as-volume.
- **Existing Godot compute-shader projects to learn from:** open Gaussian-splatting compute rasterizers — 2Retr0/GodotGaussianSplatting (tile-based rasterizer entirely in RenderingDevice compute), haztro/godot-gaussian-splatting (compute preprocess + radix-sort), and ReconWorldLab/gdgs (MIT, CompositorEffect) — demonstrate the exact RenderingDevice compute + sort + composite patterns you’ll reuse, even though they render splats not LBM fields.

### Subsystem 3 — Gaussian-Splat Room Capture → Domain: HIGHEST RISK / phase it

- **Capture UX is mature and cheap.** As of early 2026, Scaniverse (Niantic, free, on-device processing), Polycam (freemium, LiDAR-enhanced), and KIRI Engine handle full phone capture→splat. Luma AI suspended in-app splat capture (pivoted to video-gen). Typical room capture: a slow guided video walkthrough (<5 cm/s), cloud processing 3–10 min (Scaniverse/Luma 3–5 min, Polycam 5–10), output 1–3M Gaussians as .ply/.splat/.spz. Scaniverse processes on-device. Niantic’s SPZ format is “freely available under an MIT license” and yields “a 90% reduction in file size compared to PLY” (a 250 MB PLY → ~25 MB). Minimum viable UX = a guided video walkthrough, not “a couple of photos” (photogrammetry needs frame overlap; textureless white walls fail without LiDAR).
- **Splat → simulation geometry is the hard part.** You need a solid/air occupancy grid (voxels) or a watertight mesh, NOT the splat cloud. Mesh extraction from splats — SuGaR (Poisson from surface-aligned Gaussians), 2DGS (TSDF fusion from oriented disks), RaDe-GS/PGSR — is fast (minutes) but the 2024–2025 literature is explicit that **real-world indoor rooms remain a significant challenge** for photometric-only methods: textureless walls, floaters, holes, broken surfaces, unclear door/window contours. Depth/normal priors (DN-Splatter; AGS-Mesh, which is smartphone-oriented) materially improve results. For an occupancy grid you can also skip meshing and threshold splat density directly, but floater removal/cleanup is mandatory.
- **Semantic segmentation (movable furniture vs. fixed walls) is Phase 2+.** LangSplat, Gaussian Grouping, and SAM-based 3D segmentation exist but are research-grade; do not commit to auto-segmentation for MVP.
- **Godot splat rendering exists but is young.** ReconWorldLab/gdgs (MIT, Godot 4.4+, CompositorEffect-based, depth-aware, `GaussianSplatNode`, imports binary .ply/.compressed.ply/.sog) is the most engine-native; 2Retr0 and haztro are compute-rasterizer viewers. All desktop/Forward+ only, with reported 4K GPU-memory-pressure glitches. Good enough to use a captured splat as a *visual backdrop* while the sim runs on a separately-derived occupancy grid.
- **Verdict / recommendation:** For MVP, decouple capture from simulation. Ship with hand-built / primitive room templates (box + draggable furniture primitives) first. Treat splat capture as a Phase 2 “import your room” feature, and even then use the splat only as visual backdrop + a *cleaned, thresholded* occupancy grid — not as authoritative geometry. Phone-cloud capture is the path of least resistance; do local RTX-class processing only if a privacy/offline requirement demands it.

### Subsystem 4 — Validation & Physics Credibility: DEFENSIBLE with disclaimers

- **Canonical validation cases to build a harness around:**
  - **Nielsen IEA Annex 20** two-dimensional ventilated-room benchmark: box room with a slot inlet, **L/H = 3.0, h/H = 0.056, Re = 5000**; “Laser-Doppler measurements and hot-wire measurements are given for comparison … both for isothermal flow and for nonisothermal flow,” with ~50 documented sub-benchmarks on Nielsen’s Aalborg University page (homes.civil.aau.dk/pvn/cfd-benchmarks). This is *the* room-airflow validation standard.
  - **Differentially heated cavity / Rayleigh-Bénard** for thermal/buoyancy validation (LBM-DDF has extensive published validation here).
  - **Lid-driven cavity** (Re=1000) for the momentum solver (FluidX3D itself uses this).
  - **Backward-facing step** for flow separation.
  - **Posner partitioned model room** (one partition, inlet/outlet) — used specifically in LBM-LES indoor validations (Molla et al.).
- **Defensible claim boundary:** LBM-LES indoor studies show “reasonable”/“satisfactory” agreement with LES and experiment for velocity and temperature *fields and patterns*, not point-precise engineering values — and Smagorinsky is over-dissipative at low Re. **Therefore market as “plausible, physically-based airflow visualization,” explicitly NOT “engineering-grade prediction.”** Reserve quantitative validation figures (e.g., velocity-profile overlays vs. Annex 20 data) for a “how accurate is this?” documentation page — that builds credibility without over-promising.
- **Additional data sources:** ASHRAE (Standard 55 thermal comfort / PMV-PPD; 62.1/62.2 ventilation rates) for boundary-condition realism and comfort overlays; SimScale’s public indoor-environment library documents realistic vent/diffuser/heat-source boundary conditions and uses CHT/LBM for buoyancy- and wind-driven room flow.

### Licensing, Steam, Competitive Landscape (brief)

- **FluidX3D license = blocker for reuse.** “Free for non-commercial use” only. You cannot ship its code (or a derivative) in a paid Steam product. Use it as a reference and benchmark oracle only; implement your own solver. Permissive reference codebase: **Autodesk XLB (Apache-2.0)**, a JAX/Python differentiable LBM — good algorithmic reference, explicitly chosen for its “permissive Apache License, in contrast to the more restrictive GPL-based licenses of other LBM libraries.” OpenLB (GPLv2), Palabos (AGPLv3), and waLBerla are copyleft and problematic for a proprietary paid release.
- **Godot is MIT** — no obstacle; you can ship a closed, paid Godot app.
- **Steam OSS precedent is strong.** Steamworks’ own documentation warns that copyleft (GPL) code combined with the Steamworks SDK is problematic unless the author grants an exception. The canonical “open-source-core, paid-convenience-binary on Steam” model is **Aseprite**: source-available on GitHub, sold on Steam/itch (US$19.99+), with a EULA that forbids redistribution of compiled binaries while allowing users to self-compile for free. Aseprite’s own FAQ frames the paid product as “the convenient .exe and .dmg files” — a proven, viable model for exactly your plan. Keep your own solver/app code under a license you control (or an Aseprite-style source-available EULA), and avoid linking any copyleft solver.
- **Competitive gap is real.** Existing tools are prosumer/engineering cloud CFD, not consumer real-time toys: **SimScale** (cloud CFD, explicit indoor-environment + LBM solver + ASHRAE 55/62.1 compliance, browser-based, subscription), **Autodesk Forma** (rapid + detailed wind analysis, AEC/site-focused), **M-Star** (GPU CFD, industrial). None offers a cheap, real-time, “drag your couch” consumer desktop experience. The market gap the product targets is confirmed.

## Recommendations (staged)

1. **Phase 0 — Solver spike (highest technical risk first).** Build the D3Q19 isothermal LBM in Godot GLSL compute, validate lid-driven cavity, and measure real MLUPs on both an RTX 3070 and a 5060 Ti. **Go/no-go threshold:** sustain ≥150 solver steps/s at 256×256×128 while rendering at ≥30 fps on the 3070. If below, drop to ~3 cm cells or a 192³ grid.
1. **Phase 1 — Add thermal + buoyancy + LES + moving geometry.** Validate the differentially-heated cavity and Annex 20 non-isothermal case. **Threshold:** qualitative match of recirculation/stratification patterns to published data; stable at target Reynolds/Rayleigh without blow-up.
1. **Phase 1 — Visualization + interaction.** Raymarch temperature/speed volume + tracer particles; drag-to-move furniture/fan/vent with live re-voxelization and DDF refill. **Threshold:** flow visibly responds to a moved object within <1 s.
1. **Phase 2 — Room import (splat).** Only after the core ships. Splat backdrop + cleaned occupancy grid; keep manual correction tools. **Threshold:** a typical living-room scan yields a watertight-enough occupancy grid after ≤2 min of user cleanup.
1. **Phase 2+ — Semantic auto-segmentation** (furniture vs. walls). Research-grade; gate behind an explicit “experimental” flag.
1. **Throughout — Credibility framing.** Ship the validation page; label the product “plausible airflow visualization,” never “engineering-grade.” Add ASHRAE-based comfort overlays as a value-add, not a compliance claim.

## Caveats

- **RTX 5060 Ti 16 GB is not in the FluidX3D benchmark table**; its throughput is inferred from its identical 448 GB/s bandwidth and the RTX 3060 Ti/3070 measurements. Confirm empirically in Phase 0.
- **Effective solver throughput with all extensions on is an estimate** (~2–4× below the isothermal FP16 benchmark). Thermal DDF, LES, re-voxelization, and shared-GPU rendering all consume bandwidth/time. Measure early rather than trusting the estimate.
- **Splat-to-geometry maturity is the single biggest schedule risk.** Indoor photometric reconstruction is explicitly called “a significant challenge” in the 2024–2025 literature; treat MVP as primitive-room-only.
- **Numerical stability of low-Mach buoyancy LBM** requires careful SI↔LBM unit conversion and small lattice velocity; instability (“white lines / blow-up”) is the classic failure mode.  Budget time for a stability/parametrization pass, and consider a TRT or regularized collision operator if pure SRT proves unstable.
- **Smagorinsky over-dissipation** in low-Re stratified flow is a known accuracy limit — fine for visualization, not for precise thermal-comfort numbers.
- **Source quality note:** Benchmark, licensing, and physics facts are drawn from primary sources (FluidX3D GitHub README, Steamworks docs, Aseprite FAQ/Wikipedia, Nielsen/Delbosc/Khan peer-reviewed LBM papers, Godot docs and issue tracker). Capture-app timing/quality figures and GPU-comparison/marketing pages (SimScale, Polycam/Scaniverse blogs, GPU spec sites) are vendor or secondary sources and should be treated as indicative, not authoritative.
